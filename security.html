One of the most significant barriers to mainstream cryptocurrency adoption is security. Unlike traditional financial institutions cryptocurrencies do not manage security centrally. These currencies expect individuals to secure their own wallets and private keys. This decentralization is indeed empowering for some users but at the same time has made users increasingly vulnerable to hacks, scams, and fraud.

In recent years, the cryptocurrency landscape has witnessed high-profile breaches and exploitations. According to Chainalysis, 2022 recorded over $3.7 billion in stolen crypto assets, mostly from decentralized finance (DeFi) platforms. While the amount dropped to $1.7 billion in 2023, the number of incidents actually rose, indicating persistent vulnerabilities. High-profile cases like the Euler Finance ($197M) and Curve Finance ($73M) exploits in 2023, and the collapse of FTX amid suspected fraud and a $600 million outflow, have shaken confidence in the ecosystem. These incidents are not limited to technical breaches. Sophisticated scams exploiting human psychology like phishing, Ponzi schemes and fake investment opportunities have surged. The Federal Trade Commission (FTC) reported over $1 billion in crypto fraud losses in the U.S. from 2021 to Q1 2022 with a median individual loss of $2,600. Scammers often use social media platforms, exploiting the emotional and cognitive vulnerabilities of users such as fear of missing out (FOMO), overconfidence or low tech literacy. Regulatory inconsistency further complicates the issue. While countries like Japan and the UK integrate crypto into existing financial systems, others like the U.S. adopt fragmented approaches, and China has issued outright bans. This lack of global alignment makes it difficult to enforce consumer protections effectively.
